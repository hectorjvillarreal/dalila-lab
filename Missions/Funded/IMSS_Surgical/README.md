## Core Research Question

How can the IMSS finance surgical care that is both timely and fiscally sustainable, given rising demand, accumulated backlogs, operating saturation, and a contributory financing structure constrained by labor market formality?

---

## The Problem

IMSS faces a structural gap between the real demand for surgical procedures and its effective capacity to finance and deliver them. Three forces are converging:

- **Demand:** Growing surgical needs driven by population aging and epidemiological transition toward chronic diseases (ischemic, cerebrovascular, cancer, trauma)
- **Costs:** Rising complexity and unit costs — specialized personnel, dedicated infrastructure, operating room time — with growing costs not matched by equivalent financing growth
- **Financing:** Dependent on formal labor market performance and contributory scheme stability — inherently constrained by Mexico's structural informality

The result: prolonged waiting times, operating room saturation, variable surgical productivity, and financial tensions affecting institutional planning.

**Regional benchmark:** Central Latin America (including Mexico) has approximately 4 operating rooms per 100,000 inhabitants, versus 25 in Western Europe — a structural infrastructure deficit compounded by demographic and epidemiological pressures.

---

## Analytical Framework

The protocol integrates three dimensions that are typically studied in isolation:

1. **Surgical demand:** current and projected procedure volumes, by diagnosis, frequency, complexity, and waiting time
2. **Provision costs:** personnel, infrastructure, OR time, inputs, medications, length-of-stay, operational efficiency
3. **Financing sources:** IMSS contributory structure, institutional budget allocations, federal transfers

The methodological bet is an integrated model comparing real data against projected surgical service supply, allowing identification of the gap between what the system needs, what it costs, and what is available to sustain it.

**Methodology:** Observational, retrospective, longitudinal with scenario projection groups. Based on IMSS administrative records and official secondary sources, 2018–2024. Selected surgical procedures and specialties (by frequency, complexity, waiting time, and institutional expenditure share). Not experimental — produces robust, policy-relevant institutional readings from real data and explicit assumptions.

**Objective:** Define possible financial strategies to reduce surgical waiting times for a representative group of diagnoses, and estimate their costs.

---

## BDH Analytical Lens

This mission is not a generic health management study. Beth's role is to anchor it in BDH's core analytical framework:

- **Contributory architecture:** IMSS is a Bismarckian institution. Surgical care financing is directly linked to payroll contributions. Any strategy that shifts financing toward general taxation must pass the fiscal sustainability test and the formalization incentive test.
- **Fiscal sustainability:** The financing gap is not purely operational — it reflects the structural constraint imposed by Mexico's low formal employment rate and chronic underinvestment in public health (≈2.6% of GDP).
- **Demographic sensitivity:** Aging accelerates both surgical demand and cost per procedure, while simultaneously eroding the contribution base. The intertemporal logic is explicit and must be modeled.
- **Five-test evaluation:** Every proposed financing strategy must be assessed for institutional feasibility, formalization incentives, fiscal sustainability, political coalition stability, and service credibility.
