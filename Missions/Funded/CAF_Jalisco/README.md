## Strategic Significance

Jalisco accounts for more than 1% of total Latin American population. A credible, replicable subnational health financing model developed here carries significant regional demonstration value. CAF's interest reflects precisely this potential: Jalisco as a laboratory for subnational health system reform in middle-income Latin America.

This mission complements BID 2, which operates at the national level (México, Costa Rica, Panamá). CAF Jalisco adds institutional granularity — subnational financing mechanisms, state-level expenditure dynamics, and the political economy of health reform below the federal level.

---

## National Backdrop

México's public health expenditure stands at approximately 2.6% of GDP — one of the lowest in the OECD and among the weakest in Latin America relative to demographic need. This chronic underinvestment creates a structural fiscal gap that BID 2 estimates widened from 3.0% to 4.8% of GDP between 2015 and 2024.

The federal trajectory has been turbulent:

- **Seguro Popular (2003–2019):** A noncontributory insurance program that significantly expanded coverage, particularly among informal workers and low-income households. Its financing was federal-state shared, with explicit per-capita transfers.
- **INSABI (2020–2022):** The replacement program launched by the López Obrador administration. Widely regarded as an institutional failure — it dismantled the Seguro Popular infrastructure without building a functional alternative. Coverage deteriorated, supply chains collapsed, and medicine shortages became systemic.
- **IMSS-Bienestar (2022–present):** The current federal vehicle, which transfers management of state health services to IMSS. Participation is voluntary at the state level. Several states have declined to join, citing concerns over loss of institutional autonomy, financing adequacy, and service quality.

---

## Jalisco's Political Economy

Jalisco is governed by Movimiento Ciudadano, an opposition party with no federal alignment with the ruling Morena coalition. The state's decision not to join IMSS-Bienestar is both a fiscal and a political choice — a rejection of federal institutional centralization and an assertion of subnational health policy autonomy.

In response, Jalisco has pursued a revival of the Seguro Popular logic: a state-managed, mixed-financing health coverage program targeting the population outside contributory social security (IMSS, ISSSTE). This represents a deliberate institutional bet — that subnational governments can design and sustain health financing models independently of the federal architecture.

The political economy dimensions are first-order analytical concerns for this mission:

- **Federal-state fiscal relations:** Jalisco's program depends on state revenues and federal health transfers (FASSA and related funds), whose adequacy is contested. The fiscal relationship between Jalisco and the federal government is structurally adversarial under the current administration.
- **Coalition stability:** The program's sustainability depends on political continuity at the state level. A change in state government could alter the institutional commitment to the model.
- **Demonstration effect and reputational stakes:** CAF's engagement raises the profile of the experiment. Success could catalyze similar subnational initiatives elsewhere in Mexico and Latin America; failure would set back the case for subnational health reform autonomy.
- **Contributory architecture tension:** Jalisco's program, like Seguro Popular before it, operates primarily outside the contributory channel. The formalization incentive implications — consistent with BDH's core analytical framework — must be assessed carefully.

## Core Analytical Questions

1. What is the long-run fiscal cost of Jalisco's health coverage program under demographic baseline projections?
2. How do chronic disease prevalence and aging interact to drive expenditure growth at the subnational level?
3. Is the current financing mechanism — state revenues plus federal transfers — sustainable over a 10–20 year horizon?
4. What financing architecture would make the program fiscally resilient without undermining formalization incentives?
5. What are the political economy conditions for coalition stability around the reform?

## Analytical Components

- Subnational fiscal-health mapping: Jalisco health expenditure linked to state budget and federal transfers
- Demographic projection: aging and chronic disease burden in Jalisco
- Expenditure dynamics: age-specific health cost profiles under demographic transition
- Financing mechanism review: FASSA, state revenues, out-of-pocket exposure
- Institutional analysis: governance structure, federal-state relations, program design
- Political economy assessment: coalition stability, replication potential, reform risks
